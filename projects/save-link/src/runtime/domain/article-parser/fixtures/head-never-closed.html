Prolog Basics Explained with Pokémon

Prolog is a logic programming language. Rather than telling the computer how to compute an answer step-by-step, you describe the world in terms of facts and rules, and the computer figures out which facts are consistent with the question you asked.

The classic introduction uses family relationships — parent of, sibling of — which is fine but a little dry. Let us instead describe the world of Pokémon to a Prolog interpreter and watch it answer questions about type matchups, evolution chains, and which moves a creature can learn.

Facts

A fact in Prolog is a statement asserted to be true. We can begin by telling Prolog about a handful of Pokémon and their primary types. The syntax is straightforward — a predicate name, followed by parenthesised arguments, terminated by a period. 

type(bulbasaur, grass).
type(charmander, fire).
type(squirtle, water).

With those three facts loaded, the interpreter knows of three creatures and their elemental affinities. Querying the database is then a matter of asking a question with one or more variables — Prolog will attempt to bind those variables to values that make the query consistent with the facts.
